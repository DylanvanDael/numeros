Reading the numbers.

Spanish numbers are mostly a pattern — learn 1 to 15 and the twenties by heart, and the rest falls into place. These are the spots that trip people up. Pin the pattern here, then drill it in the game.

Spelling & accents

Only four numbers hide a written accent

Among the teens and twenties, the accent lands exactly on the ones built from seis, dós and trés. Nowhere else.

16 dieciséis 22 veintidós 23 veintitrés 26 veintiséis

16–29 are one word; 30 and up split into three

The teens and twenties are written solid. From thirty on, you spell them out with y between the tens and the units. The old two-word forms are archaic.

veintiuno one word treinta y uno three diez y seis → dieciséis

The word y only sits between tens and units

It never appears after the hundreds. So there's an y in treinta y cinco, but none right after ciento.

105 ciento cinco (no y) 135 ciento treinta y cinco ciento y cinco
The hundreds

cien for exactly 100, ciento for 101–199

On its own, and before a bigger unit, it's cien. The moment something smaller follows, it becomes ciento.

100 cien 101 ciento uno 100 000 cien mil 100 000 000 cien millones

Three of the hundreds are irregular

Most follow the pattern (doscientos, trescientos…), but 500, 700 and 900 don't. Memorise these three directly.

500 quinientos cincocientos 700 setecientos sietecientos 900 novecientos nuevecientos
Grammar that changes the word

uno shortens to un before a masculine noun

When you actually count something masculine, uno drops its -o. And 21 picks up an accent as veintiún.

un día veintiún años treinta y un libros

“One” and the hundreds agree in gender

Before a feminine noun, the ending flips to -a: una, veintiuna, and the hundreds too — doscientas, quinientas.

una casa veintiuna personas doscientas casas doscientos
Thousands & millions

mil takes no “un”, and doesn't pluralise as a count

1,000 is simply mil — never “un mil”. As a multiplier it stays mil: dos mil, cien mil. (The plural miles only means a vague “thousands of”.)

1000 mil un mil 2000 dos mil 100 000 cien mil cien miles

millón is a noun — it pluralises and needs de

Unlike mil, a million behaves like a real noun: un millón, dos millones, and it takes de before whatever you're counting.

un millón de euros tres millones de personas but: mil euros (no “de”)
Reading & writing figures

The decimal point and thousands separator are swapped

In Spain and much of the Spanish-speaking world it's the reverse of English: a comma marks decimals, a period (or a thin space) groups thousands. Some Latin American countries follow the English style instead — so read the context.

1.000 = one thousand 3,14 = three point one four
Days & months

They're lowercase, and only two carry an accent

Unlike English, Spanish days and months are written in lowercase mid-sentence. Among the days, only miércoles and sábado take a written accent; none of the months do.

lunes, not “Lunes” miércoles · sábado accents

The seven days — los días de la semana

The Spanish week starts on Monday.

lunes Monday martes Tuesday miércoles Wednesday jueves Thursday viernes Friday sábado Saturday domingo Sunday

The twelve months — los meses del año

Close cousins of the English names — septiembre is also written setiembre.

enero January febrero February marzo March abril April mayo May junio June julio July agosto August septiembre September octubre October noviembre November diciembre December

Now make it stick

Rules fade; reps don't. Try reverse mode to go from Spanish back to English or digits, and let the game re-serve whatever you miss until the irregular hundreds — and the accented days — feel automatic.
